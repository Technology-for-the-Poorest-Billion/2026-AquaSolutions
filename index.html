The problem

Harare's invisible water crisis

80% of water consumption in Harare, Zimbabwe comes from unregulated boreholes — with no monitoring infrastructure to detect contamination. When faecal contamination enters the groundwater, waterborne disease outbreaks can spread silently through communities before anyone knows the source.

Allen Chafa's Smart Water Tech had a sensor-based chlorine injection system, but the fuzzy logic control algorithm was fragile: it collapsed on single sensor failure, and chlorine dosing is high-stakes — too much is toxic, too little leaves the water unsafe.

Our solution

A report-labelled water safety pipeline

Rather than trying to dose chlorine automatically, we shifted the problem: build a system that monitors water quality, collects community illness reports, and uses machine learning to flag contamination risk. Three components work together.

📡

Sensor Node

Low-cost ESP32 field nodes measure pH, turbidity, ORP, UV absorbance, and chlorine residual at the borehole every 15 minutes.

The key innovation

Turning illness reports into training labels

No lab data exists linking sensor readings to real illness outcomes. Our solution was to design a labelling algorithm that converts community illness reports into weighted risk scores — replacing expensive lab testing with community signal.

1
7-day rolling window

Collect all illness reports for each sensor station within 7 days — based on WHO cholera incubation (up to 5 days) plus 2 days reporting lag.

2
Logarithmic decay scoring

Each report scored using 1 × (1 − log(t+1) / log(8)). Recent reports carry more weight — most cases present within 48 hours.

3
Confidence tier binning

Scores summed and binned into discrete tiers (0.3 / 0.6 / 0.9). Unlabelled periods treated as unknown — not safe.

4
XGBoost trains on labelled data

Confidence scores used as sample weights during training, giving higher-certainty labels more influence on the model.

Absence of reports ≠ safe water

Unlabelled periods retain risk — we deliberately excluded absence of reports as a positive safety signal to avoid label poisoning.

Core assumption

Illness reports are used as a contamination proxy. Noise from point-of-use recontamination and non-waterborne illness is an acknowledged limitation requiring field validation.

Results

What we built and tested

The ML pipeline was validated on synthetic data as a proof of concept. Near-perfect classification on the synthetic test set confirms the approach works — real-world performance depends on field data accumulating through the reporting system.

Confusion Matrix

Near-perfect classification on synthetic test data — XGBoost outperformed logistic regression and always-majority baselines.

SHAP Feature Importance

pH is the strongest predictor, consistent with domain knowledge. Positive SHAP values push toward a risk classification.

User interface

Built on Zimbabwe's existing health infrastructure

Rather than building a parallel system, we integrated with DHIS2 — Zimbabwe's national health information platform, already used by Village Health Workers across the country. The dashboard gives government officials a real-time view of borehole status and illness reports across Harare.

Why it works

Designed for the real world

Report from anywhere

Dashboard or SMS — anyone can flag illness without visiting a clinic or owning a smartphone.

No lab costs for labels

Illness reports generate training labels automatically, replacing expensive and slow lab water testing.

Immediate offline inference

Once trained, the model runs directly on the sensor node — no internet required, no lab turnaround wait.

Open source, ready to hand over
